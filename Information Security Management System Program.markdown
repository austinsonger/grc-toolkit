# **Purpose**

The purpose of this document is to define the scope of the Information Security Management System (ISMS) at [Company]. This includes delineating the boundaries within which the ISMS operates, specifying inclusions and exclusions, and identifying the organizational units, processes, locations, technologies, networks, and IT infrastructure involved. The document aims to provide a clear framework for managing and protecting [Company]'s information assets, ensuring they are secure from unauthorized access, disclosure, alteration, destruction, or loss. By establishing this scope, [Company] commits to preserving the confidentiality, integrity, and availability of its information assets, in alignment with ISO/IEC 27001 standards and other applicable legal and regulatory requirements.

# **Definition Scope of ISMS**

The Organization needs to define the boundaries of its ISMS to decide which information it wants to protect. Such information must be protected regardless of if it is stored, processed, or transferred in or out of the ISMS scope. The fact that some information is available outside of the scope doesn't mean the security measures won't apply to it – this only means that the responsibility for applying the security measures will be transferred to a third party who manages that information.

# **Information Security Policy**

## ***Objectives of the ISMS***

General objectives for the information security management system are the following:

1. Demonstrate management commitment to, and support for, information security;
2. Establish directives and principles for action with regards to information security;
3. Ensure alignment with applicable legal and regulatory requirements;

## **Information Security Program Charter**

### ***Mission***

The mission of the Information Security Program is to appropriately preserve the confidentiality, integrity and availability of the Organization’s information assets in accordance with the *Information Security Policy*. The Information Security Program serves as the Organization’s mechanism to appropriately identify, select, maintain and improve information security controls.

The purpose of the Information Security Program Charter is the establishment and maintenance of the Information Security Program through a risk-based approach and a continual improvement lifecycle. Operating areas will implement and ensure conformance with the Information Security Program.

## ***Scope***

### ***Personnel***

All information owners, information custodians and authorized users of the Organization’s information assets falling within the scope of the Information Security Program.

- An information owner is defined as one who has primary responsibility for particular information on behalf of the Company. Managers of functional areas are the owners of information and information systems on behalf of the Organization, and as such are responsible for their use and protection.
- An information custodian is defined as one who manages information resources on behalf of an information owner. Information custodians are in physical or logical possession of information. This possession does not imply authorization.
- An authorized user is defined as an employee, consultant, contractor, business partner or other individual who has been granted access to the Organization’s information or assets.
- An information asset is defined as information, in any form, created, collected or stored to support the Organization’s business operations, whether belonging or entrusted to the Organization.

### ***Applicability to Other Program(s)***

The protection of information assets to which the Organization has stewardship responsibilities is not the sole responsibility of the Information Security Program. To create a holistic and secure environment, multiple programs must work together with clearly defined responsibilities.

## **Governance of Program**

### **ISMS Management Committee**

The ISMS Management Committee, led by the IT and Security team, is responsible for overseeing the Information Security Program, its objectives, goals, and enforcement. This committee will review the Information Security Program at planned intervals, which will occur as frequently as necessary but no less than once per year. The ISMS Management Committee takes a strategic view of information security, provides strategic direction to the ISMS Management Committee, and is responsible for the final determination of risk acceptance or mitigation in case of discrepancies between the Information Security Program and operating areas.

The responsibilities of the ISMS Management Committee include:

- Demonstrating support for the Information Security Program by approving the Information Security Policy and ISMS Governance Plan.
- Allocating budget and resources to Information Security Program initiatives.
- Ensuring conformance with all ISMS requirements, policies, standards, and procedures.
- Holding permanent membership on the ISMS Management Committee.
- Performing an annual Management Review using the review criteria specified in Clause 9.3 of the ISO 27001:2022 standard.
- Formulating Corrective Action Plans to improve the Program.

The IT and Security team shall serve as the organiser and chair of the ISMS Management Committee. The ISMS Manager will be responsible for reporting on the ISMS and will also serve as the record-keeper, taking meeting minutes and ensuring they are formally transcribed and entered into the ISMS document and record repository.

The ISMS Management Committee will meet no less than once per year to review the ISMS but may meet on a more frequent basis during the year if deemed appropriate.

Additional details on the operation, responsibilities, and membership of the ISMS Management Committee can be found in the ISMS Management Committee Charter later in this document.

Input from the ISMS Management Committee on policies and other documents will be sought for implementation and major revision releases with final approval authority residing with the VP of Engineering. Minor releases or non-substantive changes may be approved by the IT and Security.

- Furnish the Information Security Program with recovery objectives;
- Validate Business Continuity plans on a regular schedule; and
- Participate in scheduled Business Continuity tests.

- Conform to, and ensure conformance with all ISMS requirements, policies, standards and procedures

### ***Underlying Principles***

The Organization ISMS is founded on two fundamental principles:

1. *Least Access*: Users shall be granted access only to those information assets necessary to perform their duties.
2. *Least Privilege*: Users shall not be permitted any more than the least privileges necessary for processing the information assets to which they have been granted access.

### **Program Risk Assessment**

### ***Process Description***

### **Input**

The input for this process is an Information Risk Assessment, which may result from either a scheduled ISMS Task Review or as needed, when a significant change occurs.

### **Activity**

The ISMS Manager will:

1. Identify the relevant information assets such as:
   1. Information
   2. Applications
   3. Computing Platforms
   4. Network Infrastructure
   5. People
   6. Facilities
2. Identify the top-level threats to Confidentiality, Integrity, and Availability
   1. Intentional or unintentional disclosure
   2. Intentional or unintentional modification
   3. Intentional or unintentional disruption
   4. Intentional or unintentional loss or destruction
3. Identify specific instances of these top-level threats
4. Identify the corresponding vulnerability which could be exploited to realise the previously identified instance of the top-level threat
5. Evaluate the probability of the exploit being realised
   1. Probability may vary for equal assets depending on location, ability, and determination of the potential attacker
6. Estimate the potential harm or impact to the organization should the exploit succeed
7. The variables identified are used to calculate inherent risk, or risk before the application of any compensating controls. Risks rated above the acceptable value of 8 (Low) will be marked for risk treatment.
8. The Risk Analyst will identify appropriate and reasonable compensating controls.
9. Compensating controls are factored in to reduce the inherent risk.
10. Compile the process parameters, controls and results into a report for management

The report is reviewed with the ISMS Management Committee either via the Communication Plan or during ISMS Management Committee scheduled meetings.

### **Risk Treatment**

### ***Risk Treatment Process Description***

### **Input**

The input for this activity is the Information Security Risk Assessment Report.

### **Activity**

If the Risk Assessment reports risk levels which may impact legal and regulatory compliance, the ISMS Manager will:

1. Obtain a legal opinion regarding the impact of the risk and compliance with law and regulation;
2. When the risk level does not impact compliance, it will be handed off to the Information Risk Phase of the process;
3. If the risk level impacts compliance, the ISMS Manager will develop treatment options and pass them along to the ISMS Management Committee for consideration;
4. Where needed, the ISMS Manager and/or the ISMS Management Committee will meet with the affected business units to discuss the treatment plan;
5. Once legal requirements have been disseminated, and feedback obtained, the ISMS Management Committee will consider the feedback and request the addition or modification of controls to achieve required compliance;
6. The added or modified controls are handed off to the documentation process for incorporation into policy or standards.

If the Risk Assessment risk levels do not impact legal or regulatory compliance, the ISMS Manager will:

1. Determine whether the risk level is acceptable:
   1. If so, the risk will be submitted to the ISMS Management Committee as Accepted.
2. If the risk level is not acceptable, the ISMS Manager will determine:
   1. If the risk can be avoided; if so, avoid it
   2. If the risk can be transferred; if so, transfer it
   3. If the risk can be remediated to an acceptable level
   4. Whether the cost of avoidance, transfer or remediation exceeds the benefit derived from the activity
   5. If the risk level can be justified for business purposes and accepted for a reasonably brief duration, it will be submitted to the ISMS Management Committee as a Conditionally Accepted Risk
3. If the risk cannot be avoided, transferred, or remediated in a cost-effective manner, it is considered an Unacceptable Risk and submitted to the ISMS Management Committee.

Once the risk level has been classified as Accepted, Conditionally Accepted, or Unaccepted, the ISMS Management Committee will:

1. Meet to formulate a formal decision on the classification;
2. Make additional recommendations or submit change request as needed;
3. In the case of Unacceptable Risk, refer the matter to the appropriate approver; depending on risk criticality, as set forth in the Risk Management Methodology; and
4. Prepare an Information Risk Treatment Plan Report for management showing final determinations, recommendations and activities.

### **Output**

The Information Risk Treatment Plan Report to the ISMS Management Committee and Management, which serves as a record of the process.

This process shall be performed at least annually or when significant changes occur to the ISMS.

If the employee who notices the nonconformity is not responsible for such nonconformity, they must forward information about that nonconformity to a responsible person/department, who must begin documenting the nonconformity in their department’s ticketing system or by emailing [Security@EXAMPLE.com](mailto:Security@EXAMPLE.com).

In order to identify the root cause of nonconformities, the Organization shall take the following factors into consideration:

- The results of the Incident Management Review Process
- Alignment of ISMS requirements with business needs and practices
- Improvements recommended as a result of the Internal Audit
- The effectiveness of existing Information Security Education materials
- The results of the most recent Risk Treatment Plan review

### ***Corrective Actions***

***Evaluating Actions to Correct Nonconformities***

In order to evaluate the efficacy of actions needed to correct identified nonconformities, the Organization shall consider the following elements:

- The business justification for change
- Resulting reduction in risk assessment levels
- Alignment with legal and regulatory requirements
- Technical and organizational feasibility of the proposed change(s)

### ***Choosing and Implementing Corrective Actions***

- Expected benefits of the action
- Direct and indirect (opportunity) costs of implementing the action
- Business need for the action
- Potential disruption to existing business processes
- Technical feasibility of the action

### ***Recording Corrective Actions***

- The source(s) utilized to identify the nonconformity
- The root cause analysis performed on the nonconformity
- The relevant business, technical, and compliance considerations utilized to evaluate the corrective action.
- Description of the action(s) taken to correct the nonconformity
- The results of the action, both positive and negative (if applicable)
- The lesson(s) learned from the corrective action

### ***Corrective Action Review***

Prior to the next scheduled review of the ISMS, corrective action results shall be reviewed and reported to the appropriate level of management. This review shall include, but will not be limited to, the following:

- Metrics regarding the effectiveness of the action
- Metrics regarding the efficiency of the action
- Identifiable results on the organization’s risk exposure
- Recommendations for any additional actions which may be needed to improve the action under review

### **A.5.1 Policies for Information Security**

The ISMS Management Committee approves all policy changes, and the Communication Plan process handles distribution to relevant personnel.

**Roles and Responsibilities.** The Cybersecurity Program Manager drafts, maintains, and coordinates policy reviews. The ISMS Management Committee approves new and revised policies. The IT and Security team handles communication and accessibility.

- Approved policy documents in the <Document Management Platform> with version history
- Policy acknowledgement records from employees
- ISMS Management Committee meeting minutes capturing review and approval decisions
- Communication records showing policy distribution

**Review and Continuous Improvement.** Policies are reviewed at least annually per the ISMS Calendar, or when significant changes occur. Effectiveness is gauged through internal audit findings, compliance assessments, and stakeholder feedback. Gaps feed into the Corrective Action Plan process.

### **A.5.3 Segregation of Duties**

*This control is also addressed in the ISMS Internal Organization section of this document.*

**Control Objective.** Separate conflicting duties and responsibilities to reduce the risk of unauthorized or accidental misuse of assets.

**Implementation.** [Company] identifies role combinations that could create opportunities for collusion, fraud, or unauthorized actions — for example, the ability to both authorize and execute the same transaction. These combinations are documented in a segregation of duties matrix.

The <Identity and Access Management Platform> enforces technical separation by blocking incompatible role assignments. Where the organization’s size makes full segregation impractical, compensating controls are applied: enhanced monitoring, supervisory review, and audit trail analysis. Any residual risk is formally documented, risk-assessed, and accepted by the appropriate owner with compensating controls clearly identified.

**Roles and Responsibilities.** The Cybersecurity Program Manager maintains the segregation matrix and identifies conflicts. The IT and Security team enforces technical restrictions through the <Identity and Access Management Platform>. Senior Administrators review role assignments within their departments.

**Evidence and Documentation.**

- Segregation of duties matrix

**Control Objective.** Synchronize clocks across all information processing systems to a single, approved time source.

**Implementation.** [Company] synchronizes all system clocks to approved, authoritative time sources using NTP (Network Time Protocol). The <Cloud Hosting Provider> provides accurate time services synchronized to national standards.

On-premises and endpoint devices synchronize to organizational time servers or directly to the cloud provider’s NTP service. Time zone and UTC usage conventions are documented. Clock accuracy is monitored, and synchronization failures trigger alerts.

Accurate and consistent timestamps are essential for log correlation (A.8.15), incident investigation (A.5.28), and forensic analysis. All log sources must produce timestamps traceable to the approved time reference.

**Roles and Responsibilities.** The IT and Security team configures and monitors time synchronization. The Cybersecurity Program Manager defines requirements. System owners verify their systems are synchronized.

- Time accuracy verification records

**Review and Continuous Improvement.** Synchronization configurations are reviewed annually and when infrastructure changes. Accuracy is verified through monitoring. Drift issues are addressed promptly through the Corrective Action Plan process.

### **A.8.18 Use of Privileged Utility Programs**

**Control Objective.** Restrict and control the use of utility programs capable of overriding system and application controls.

**Implementation.** [Company] restricts access to system utilities that could bypass security controls — disk editors, registry editors, debug tools, network sniffers, password recovery tools, and similar programs. These utilities are removed from systems where not needed and, where required, restricted to authorized personnel only.

Use of privileged utilities requires authorization from the Cybersecurity Program Manager, is limited to specific tasks, and is logged comprehensively. Utilities are not available on standard user endpoints; they are accessible only on designated administrative workstations or through controlled provisioning.

The separation of privileged utility access from normal operational access reduces the risk of accidental misuse or exploitation by attackers who compromise a standard account.

**Roles and Responsibilities.** The IT and Security team controls utility availability and access. The Cybersecurity Program Manager authorizes use. System administrators use utilities only for approved purposes and document their actions.

**Evidence and Documentation.**

- Authorized utility inventory
- Access restriction configurations
- Usage authorization records
- Utility usage logs
- Administrative workstation configurations

**Review and Continuous Improvement.** Utility restrictions are reviewed annually. Unauthorized utility presence is detected through endpoint scanning. Usage logs are reviewed for anomalies. Findings are addressed through the Corrective Action Plan process.

### **A.8.22 Segregation of Networks**

**Control Objective.** Segregate groups of information services, users, and information systems based on trust levels and business requirements.

**Implementation.** [Company] segregates networks to limit the blast radius of security incidents and enforce access control at the network layer. Segregation is implemented through virtual private clouds, subnets, security groups, and network access control lists on <Cloud Hosting Provider>.

Key segregation boundaries include: production vs. non-production environments, internet-facing vs. internal services, management networks vs. data networks, and high-sensitivity zones vs. general-purpose zones. Traffic between segments is controlled by security groups and firewalls enforcing least-privilege rules.

The segregation design supports the principle of defense in depth — even if one segment is compromised, lateral movement to other segments is restricted. Network monitoring (A.8.16) detects unauthorized cross-segment traffic.

**Roles and Responsibilities.** The IT and Security team implements and maintains segregation. The Cybersecurity Program Manager defines segregation requirements. Network changes affecting segmentation go through change management with security review.

**Review and Continuous Improvement.** Segregation is reviewed annually and after infrastructure changes. Penetration testing validates effectiveness. Rule sets are reviewed for necessity. Findings are addressed through the Corrective Action Plan process.
